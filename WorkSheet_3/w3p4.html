Explanation

When we reduce the scaling factor, the texture get magnified. The bigger the magnification the less detailed the texture becomes.
Comparing linear vs nearest we observe that linear interpolates the different colours in the texture, such that the texture appears smoother.
When using nearest the result is more pixilated and there is no smooth transition from one colour to another.

The scaling factor amplifies the effects of the filtering methods. When using a low scaling factor the difference between nearest and linear filtering becomes obvious.
Whereas with a high scaling factor the difference is more subtle.

The pixel subdivision level together with linear filtering does not affect the texture aliasing much.
The reason for this is that linear filtering already interpolates the colours in the texture.
However the pixel subdivison level together with nearest filtering affects the texture aliasing quite a bit.
When using a high pixel subdivison level with nearest filtering the colours in the texture gets more interpolated, meaning less aliasing.
When using a low pixel subdivison level with nearest filtering there is no change compared to using just nearest filtering.
Since the subdivision is what causes the subdivision.

To get the least amount of aliasing we would use a high pixel subdivison level along with linear filtering.
